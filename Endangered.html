Critically Endangered

Amur leopards have a wild population of less than 100 individuals, mostly due to human activities, including poaching, forest fires, inbreeding, and disease. Amur leopards hunt a wide variety of animals, normally at night, including deer, badgers and hares.

Hawksbill turtles inhabit tropical and some subtropical regions in the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans. Turtle populations have declined more than 80% over the last century, primarily due to trade of the carapace (shell), called “tortoiseshell.”

Only found in Mexico, the Axolotl, also known as the 'Mexican Walking Fish,' is almost extinct due to invasive species being introduced into its habitat. Axolotl have the ability to regenerate any body organ or lost limb up to 5 times.

Endangered

Deforestation, a human cause, is the biggest threat to red pandas, causing habitat destruction, fragmentation and the population of red pandas to decline by 50%. Red pandas have adapted to extremely high altitudes and prefer mountain habitats.

Sea lions are vulnerable to pollution, climate change, bycatch, and diseases introduced by other species resulting in them being endangered with only 18,000 of them today. Sea lions are known for their intelligence, playfulness and noisy barking.

Known for its spotted coat and bat-like ears, the African wild dog is one of the planet’s most endangered mammals. African wild dogs require abundant space and are highly susceptible to habitat fragmentation, conflicting with livestock farmers.

Vulnerable

The black spider monkey population is in decline primarily due to loss of suitable habitat and illegal hunting. They are known for there strong tails and missing thumbs.

Iguanas are among the largest lizards in the Americas. Much of the marine Iguana population loss is due to rats and feral cats on the island, making them highly vulnerable.

The capybara is a semiaquatic mammal and the world’s largest rodent and is a ver social creatur.However, their populations have declined in some areas due to over hunting.
